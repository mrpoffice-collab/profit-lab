The findings

1. $3,256 of finished work is sitting unbilled

Every July visit is complete, and none of it has been invoiced. This isn’t a pricing problem — it’s money already earned, waiting on a Send button.

Do this: invoice all completed July work today.

Worth: $3,256 now

2. Spring cleanups are your busiest low-earner: $47/hour

Fourteen flat-rate cleanups at ~$300 each consumed 71 crew hours — $47/hour, 42% below your blended rate, during the exact weeks you were also starting mowing routes. Flat pricing is absorbing every extra hour those properties demand.

Do this: next spring, quote cleanups at $75/crew-hour with a $300 minimum, or raise flat rates ~60%. At this season’s volume that’s the same work for $2,350 more.

Worth: ~$2,350/season

3. One client earns you $29/hour: Harold Jenkins

His $50 weekly mow takes 1.75 hours — every week. That’s 65% below your mowing average. Over the season, his 24.5 hours brought in $700; the same hours at your average rate earn $1,985.

Do this: reprice to $90–$95/visit to reflect the lot, or hand the stop to a competitor with a smile.

Worth: ~$1,270/season

4. A grandfathered rate that never grew up: Dorothy Mills at $35/hour

Her biweekly $35 rate is 57% below average and has clearly never been raised. Loyal clients deserve fair prices — in both directions.

Do this: move her to $50–$55/visit with a friendly note. Worst case, you free up a route slot at rush hour.

Worth: ~$370/season

5. Mulch jobs look big but bill materials at cost

$7,370 of mulch work carried $2,948 of materials you passed through with zero markup — so a headline $110/hour becomes $66/hour after the supplier gets paid.

Do this: line-item materials with a standard 30% markup. Nobody blinks; it’s how every contractor prices.

Worth: ~$880/season

6. Your best-kept secret is fertilization: $114/hour

Eleven clients on the 3-round program produced your highest per-hour earnings — 40% above blended — in half-hour visits that slot between mows. You have ~10 more mowing clients not on the program, and you’re already on their lawns.

Do this: offer every mowing client the program at spring signup. Ten conversions ≈ $2,250/season at your best margin.

Worth: ~$2,250/season

Bottom line

Nothing above requires new customers, new equipment, or more hours. It’s the same season, priced like the data says it should be: roughly $7,100 more per season, plus $3,256 collected now. On a $39,340 season, that’s an 18% raise for zero extra work — which is typical: industry surveys find most service businesses unknowingly underprice by 10–30%.
